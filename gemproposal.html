02 What you have today (in plain English)

Before the technical roadmap, here's exactly what's running right now at https://gem-web-production.up.railway.app, in non-technical terms.

★ Today's setup, explained like you're not an engineer

Imagine GEM is a really smart receptionist at a desk. When someone walks up and asks a question, three things happen:

  1. The face and voice (HeyGen): The receptionist's appearance, voice, and lip-sync. We "rent" this from a company called HeyGen. They handle the avatar streaming so we don't have to.
  2. The brain (Anthropic's Claude): When a visitor asks a question, we send it to Anthropic's Claude — one of the most powerful AI models in the world. Claude reads a one-page "cheat sheet" we wrote about the City of Colton (basic info — address, phone, hours, common services) and writes an answer.
  3. The follow-up (Twilio): If the visitor wants a copy of the answer texted to their phone, we send it via Twilio (the same company that powers Uber's texts).

The problem with the cheat-sheet approach: the receptionist only knows what's on the one page. If you ask something not on the page, they guess (sometimes well, sometimes not). And every question we send to Claude costs us a small amount of money.

This is fine for the demo. To turn it into a real product where the City of Colton — or a gym chain, or Randall Lewis's real estate company — can sell GEM to their visitors, we need to upgrade two things specifically: where the knowledge comes from (Section 5, "RAG") and where the brain runs (Section 5, "Own Inference"). Both are explained in layman's terms in Section 6.

05 Staged technical roadmap (with the why)

Each phase is independent — we ship Phase 1 as v1, then trigger Phase 2/3/4 based on traction. The phases exist because doing everything at once costs ~$50K up front and most of it would be premature optimization. Staging spreads the spend and forces each upgrade to be triggered by real demand.

PHASE 1 · v1 MVP LAUNCH · INCLUDED IN BUILD FEE

Launch the multi-tenant platform with the cheap, fast brain

Ship date: end of build

What we ship

  • HeyGen avatar + 4-screen kiosk runtime (already built, gets polished)
  • Swap Claude → Groq Llama 3.3 70B for the brain
  • Multi-tenant dashboard, Stripe billing, basic analytics
  • All 6 languages, voice + text input, SMS handoff
  • Per-tenant Markdown knowledge editor — paste in hours, policies, FAQs; injected into the system prompt at request time

Why this is the minimum viable launch

The current demo is single-tenant and uses Claude (expensive, slow). Without multi-tenancy, Brandon's sales team has to call us every time they close a deal. Without the Groq swap, every question costs ~$0.01 and takes ~2s instead of ~$0.0005 and ~300ms — at scale that's the difference between 80% and 30% gross margin.

Why Groq specifically (not Claude, not OpenAI, not self-hosted)

  • Runs Llama on custom LPU chips at 500+ tokens/sec — the avatar starts speaking almost the instant the user finishes
  • Per-question cost is 20× lower than Claude with no quality drop for this use case
  • Still a managed API, so we don't have GPUs to babysit
  • Easy to swap for self-hosted later (Phase 3) without changing app code — same OpenAI-compatible interface

What happens if we skip it

Brandon's per-kiosk margin caps at ~75% instead of 85%, response latency feels laggy on iPad, and every new client needs an engineer to onboard.

Result

~$180/kiosk/mo, sub-second responses, clients can update their own content without us.

Optional v1 add-on — Lite RAG ($2,000): Upgrades the Markdown editor to handle ~80K chars per tenant with simple chunked retrieval. Worth it only if a launch client has substantial content (a real city policy doc, gym FAQ binder) out the gate.
PHASE 2 · FULL RAG INGESTION · $8,000 ADD-ON

Upgrade the "cheat sheet" into a full library card

Trigger: ~Month 4, or first client who wants to upload PDFs

What we ship

  • Neon Postgres with pgvector as the per-tenant knowledge store
  • PDF/URL/map ingest pipeline
  • Automatic chunking, semantic retrieval, citation tracking ("according to Section 4.2 of the Municipal Code…")
  • Unlimited per-tenant content; instant updates by re-indexing a single doc

Why this phase exists

The Markdown editor in Phase 1 maxes out around 50–80K characters per tenant. For a small gym or single-purpose kiosk that's plenty. But the moment a real client (City of Colton's full policy library, Randall Lewis's building portfolio with floor plans + lease docs + amenity guides) wants to upload PDFs, that's RAG territory. Without it, GEM caps at small clients only.

Why now and not in v1

RAG is engineering work that takes 80+ hours to do right. Doing it before you have a paying client who needs it = $8K of risk on a feature nobody asked for. Doing it after = the first client who needs it pays for it via your Pro+ tier upgrade.

What it unlocks commercially

  • The "Pro+" tier ($1,750/mo) — same ~85% gross margin, $500 more per client per month
  • Enterprise sales motion (cities, real estate developers) — these clients literally ask "can we upload our docs" in the first meeting
  • Citations — the avatar can say "this answer is from Section 4 of the Municipal Code" which is a huge trust win in government settings

What happens if we skip it

GEM stays a "smart FAQ" product instead of a "knowledge platform." Caps your TAM at small clients with simple content.

PHASE 3 · OWN THE INFERENCE · $6,000 ADD-ON

Stop renting the AI brain — buy our own

Trigger: ~Month 6, or once you have 10+ paying clients

What we ship

  • Deploy vLLM or SGLang on Northflank or RunPod Serverless ($2.65–3/hr H100)
  • Move from per-token API spend to per-second GPU spend
  • Same model interface, just different infrastructure under the hood

Why this phase exists

At ~10+ active kiosks, you're paying Groq enough per month that running your own GPU becomes cheaper. The math: Groq charges ~$0.0005/question. A serverless H100 at $2.74/hr can handle ~50 questions/sec = $0.000015/question. That's 30× cheaper at the unit level, but you only beat Groq on total cost once your fleet has enough volume to keep the GPU busy.

Why we wait for the trigger

A GPU sitting idle still costs $2.74/hr. Under 10 kiosks, the GPU spends most of its time waiting, and you'd actually lose money vs Groq. The break-even (10–15 kiosks) is where serverless utilization crosses the line.

What it unlocks commercially

  • The business gets more profitable per client as you grow, not less — opposite of most API-dependent SaaS
  • At 30 kiosks, ~$4,500/mo in savings that drops straight to the bottom line
  • You stop being dependent on Groq's pricing decisions — if they raise prices, you don't care
  • Faster custom fine-tunes become possible (you control the inference stack end-to-end)

What happens if we skip it

Per-kiosk LLM cost stays at ~$0.50–1.50/mo instead of ~$0.20. Bearable, but you leave ~$300/kiosk/yr on the table indefinitely.

! Two things to be clear about

(1) We are NOT training a foundation model. Every phase uses an existing open-weight model (Meta's Llama, Alibaba's Qwen, etc.) that someone else already spent $50M+ training and released free. "Build your own LLM" = self-host an existing model + add your knowledge. Training from scratch is a $10M–$1B exercise nobody outside Meta/Anthropic/OpenAI does.

(2) Fine-tuning ≠ knowledge. Fine-tuning changes how the model speaks; RAG controls what it knows. Facts go in the KB. Tone goes in fine-tuning (and only when needed, which is rare).

06 Key concepts in layman's terms

The two terms Brandon and his stakeholders will hear most often are "RAG" and "own inference." Here's what they actually mean, without the jargon.

RAG — the receptionist with a library card

★ What "RAG" actually means

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Forget the name — here's the analogy:

Today (no RAG): Imagine our receptionist memorized a one-page cheat sheet about Colton — address, hours, common services. When someone asks "what's the late fee on a building permit?" and it's not on the cheat sheet, the receptionist guesses (or says "I don't know"). The cheat sheet maxes out at about 80,000 characters because that's all that fits in their head at once.

With RAG: We give the receptionist a library card. They no longer memorize anything. Instead, the moment someone asks a question, they look it up in your full library — every PDF, every webpage, every policy document you've uploaded. They find the relevant pages in under a second, read them, and write an answer based on what they found. They can even cite the source: "According to Section 4.2 of the Municipal Code, late fees on permit applications are…"

Without RAG (today)
Smart guessing

Avatar relies on a small "cheat sheet" written into its instructions. Anything beyond that, it guesses or refuses.

Good for: a small gym with one page of FAQs.

Bad for: a city with 200 pages of policy, hours that change weekly, dozens of departments.

With RAG (Phase 2)
Looks it up live

Avatar searches your full document library at question time. Pulls the most relevant 2–3 paragraphs, reads them, writes a citation-backed answer.

Good for: any client with real content — cities, real estate portfolios, multi-location franchises.

Bonus: clients update their own knowledge by uploading new PDFs — no engineer needed.

Why "RAG" gets mentioned constantly in 2026: it's how every serious AI product handles client-specific knowledge. ChatGPT does it with their "Custom GPTs." Notion does it with "Notion AI Q&A." It's the standard way to make a general-purpose AI know about your specific business.

Own inference — buying the engine instead of renting

★ What "own inference" actually means

"Inference" is the technical word for running an AI to answer a question. Right now we're renting inference from Groq (or Anthropic): they own the powerful servers, we send them a question, they charge us a few cents and send back an answer.

"Own inference" means we rent a powerful GPU server ourselves (or eventually buy one), put the AI model on it, and run it for our own clients without going through Groq.

The hotel-vs-house analogy:

  • Renting (today): Pay-per-night at a hotel. Cheap if you only travel occasionally. Expensive if you're there every day.
  • Owning (Phase 3): Pay a fixed monthly mortgage for a house. Higher minimum cost. But the more nights you spend there, the cheaper each night becomes.

The math: a GPU server costs about $2,000/month whether we use it 1 hour or 700 hours. Below ~10 kiosks, Groq is cheaper. Above ~10–15 kiosks, owning beats renting by about 3×.

Importantly: we use the same AI model either way. Same Llama. Same quality. Just running on different hardware that we control.

Renting (Phase 1 & 2)
Pay-per-question via Groq

$0.0005 per question. No fixed cost. Scales automatically. Perfect while you're growing from 1 → 10 kiosks.

Downside: margin gets squeezed as volume grows — every question costs you money.

Owning (Phase 3)
Pay-per-second-of-GPU on Northflank

~$400/mo fixed for a GPU server that handles 30+ kiosks. ~$0.000015/question at scale — about 30× cheaper per unit.

Upside: the more clients you have, the lower your cost per client becomes.

"Training your own LLM" — what people actually mean

! The "build your own AI" confusion

When someone says "we should build our own LLM," they could mean three completely different things at three completely different price points:

What people sayWhat it actually meansReal cost
"Train our own LLM from scratch"Build a Claude or Llama from zero. PhDs, trillions of tokens, months of GPU time$10M–$1B
"Fine-tune our own model"Take an existing model (Llama) and teach it your style or refusal rules$200–$10,000
"Self-host an open model"Run Meta's free Llama on your own GPUs and plug in your knowledge (RAG)$0–$1,000 setup

What we're doing in Phase 2 + 3 is the third one. We never train a foundation model. Nobody outside Meta, Anthropic, OpenAI, and a few others does. When Brandon's Colorado engineer friends said "build your own to save on API costs," they meant Tier 3 — self-host Llama, add your knowledge — which is exactly what's in this roadmap.

Why we start on Railway

It works, the URL is live, and Brandon can demo to clients today. Migrating before there's a real reason burns engineering hours on something users never see.

Why we migrate to Fly.io at Phase 2

  • HeyGen uses WebRTC/LiveKit — Fly's VM model handles persistent connections natively (no weird drops)
  • Multi-region: kiosks in Florida, Texas, California each hit a nearby server (lower latency, snappier avatar)
  • Postgres at scale is ~3× cheaper on Fly than Railway ($34/mo vs $92/mo for comparable specs)
  • Scale-to-zero on per-tenant preview/staging environments saves real money as tenant count grows
  • Migration effort already scoped into the Phase 2 budget (~6–8 hours)

Why Cloudflare R2 for file storage (not S3)

$0 egress fees. When tenants download their own uploaded files (PDFs, branding assets), S3 would charge per gigabyte. R2 is pennies for the same workload.

11 Revenue split waterfall (per client deal)

  1. Operational cost paid first (off the top, passthrough)
  2. Management retainer (Options B and C only)
  3. Remainder split per agreed equity %
  4. Sales commission out of Brandon's side (10–20% to whoever closed)
⌘ Why this order

Infrastructure has to be paid no matter what — that comes off the top. The retainer keeps us actively maintaining the system. The remaining profit is what equity holders actually share. Sales commission comes out of the deal-owner's share because commissions are an acquisition cost specific to that deal.
