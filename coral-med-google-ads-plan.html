01 · Goal & structure

One campaign. Two ad groups. Nothing else.

The single goal is new patient acquisition. We launch one Search campaign — no Display, no Performance Max. PMax leaks budget into low-intent placements, and Google's Local Services Ads don't cover medical practices, so Search is where the real intent lives.

At $500/month (~$16/day), spend has to be concentrated, not spread. Three ad groups would starve each other of data, so we run two and park the third. One campaign also keeps reporting simple: one line item, one cost, one count of new patients.

02 · Geography, schedule & budget

Where, when, and how much

Geo: a tight 5–6 mile radius around the office — Huntington Beach core, Fountain Valley, and Westminster. At this budget, closer is better: nearer patients convert at higher rates and cost the same per click. The radius widens in phase 2, not now.

Schedule: office hours only — roughly 8am–5pm weekdays. The primary conversion is a phone call, and after-hours calls currently have no capture path while Nat is offline. Every dollar runs while someone can actually answer the phone. Once the message-taker rebuild of Nat is live, the schedule opens up and after-hours ad calls become captured leads instead of lost ones.

04 · Conversion tracking & compliance

Measure without touching PHI

Calls: track via Google's call-extension conversions with a 60-second threshold — no third-party call tracker that touches PHI. If ad calls route through RingCentral, the existing BAA question applies here as well.

Forms & bookings: the landing page CTA points at a GHL form or booking widget, covered by the active HIPAA add-on. The Google Ads conversion fires on the thank-you page view. No condition or health details in URLs, and nothing health-related passed back to Google.

Pipeline: ad leads enter GHL as genuinely new contacts, so the Welcome SMS automation fires on them automatically once LC Phone is live. Every ad lead gets a "Google Ads" source tag so reporting shows cost per new patient — not just cost per click.

Hard compliance line

No remarketing. No customer-list audiences. No site-visitor retargeting of any kind. Under current HHS guidance on tracking technologies, retargeting visitors of a medical practice website is a genuine liability — this stays off permanently, not just at launch.

05 · Landing page

Don't send traffic to the homepage

One dedicated new-patient page, in this order: same-day availability above the fold, a click-to-call button, the accepted-insurance list, Dr. Moussa's photo with a one-line bio, the GHL booking form, then address and map. At a small budget the landing page matters even more — with only 35–70 clicks a month, each one has to count. A focused page like this can double conversion rate versus the generic homepage.

06 · First 60 days

Launch, prune, shift

Weeks 1–2

Launch. Review the search-terms report every 2–3 days and prune junk queries aggressively into the negative list — at $16/day, one bad query type can eat a week of budget.

Weeks 3–6

Data accrues slower at this spend, so give keywords 6 weeks, not 4. Pause anything with 20+ clicks and zero conversions. Promote proven winners to exact match.

Month 2

Shift budget toward the winning ad group. If cost per inquiry lands under ~$150, present the case to Dr. Moussa for scaling toward $1,000/month and reintroducing the same-day ad group.

Insurance in the ads

Accepted plans — Cigna, Blue Cross, United Healthcare — get named directly in callouts and rotated into headlines ("Accepting Cigna, Blue Cross & UHC"). Insurance is the top deciding factor in this vertical, and naming plans in the ad pre-qualifies clicks so out-of-network searchers don't spend the budget. The same list goes above the fold on the landing page.
